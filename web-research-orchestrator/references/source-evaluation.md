# Source Evaluation

Use source metadata and confidence notes throughout the research. Prefer primary and authoritative sources, but include secondary sources when they help discover controversies, market framing, or practical context.

## Source priority

High priority:

- Official documentation and standards
- Government and regulator pages
- Academic papers and peer-reviewed literature
- Company filings, earnings materials, product docs, press releases
- Direct transcripts, speeches, interviews, and first-party statements

Medium priority:

- Reputable news organizations
- Industry analysts and trade publications
- Books, conference talks, and expert blogs with clear authorship

Low priority:

- Aggregators, anonymous posts, social media, SEO summaries, unsourced videos, marketing pages without evidence

## Metadata to capture

For each important source, record:

- `url`
- `title`
- `author_or_org`
- `published_or_updated`
- `retrieved_at`
- `source_type`
- `claims_supported`
- `evidence_excerpt_or_summary`
- `confidence`: high, medium, or low
- `caveat`

## Verification rules

- Cross-check important facts.
- For time-sensitive information, verify the publication date and whether the page was updated.
- For numbers, include unit, period, geography, and method when available.
- For claims from interested parties, label the interest and seek independent confirmation.
- Do not convert correlation, marketing language, or expert opinion into established fact.
- Keep direct quotes short; paraphrase most content.

## Confidence guidance

- `high`: primary/official source, current, directly supports the claim.
- `medium`: credible secondary source, or primary source with some missing context.
- `low`: indirect source, unclear date/authorship, single-source claim, or unverifiable media summary.
